Flow Trails

I was inspired to start this project after seeing other programmers make procedural/generative designs using code.
The purpose of this was to test how vector fields could manipulate objects in space as they moved across the plane,
and see what behaviors arose from an aggregate of forces. Each canvas initializes a thousand objects whose
physical properties are constantly being updated in relation to the force at each point. It takes some simple vector
operations to get this working, but the end result is spectacular. Above is a screenshot of how the forces guide the
objects' paths over time. I added some nice trails to add some nice flow-y-ness to it.
Kind of reminds me of a seabed of anemone.



Truchet Tiles

I learned about truchet tiles about a year ago when I was watching a youtuber make these from scratch.
Basically, these are tiles that are rotationally asymmetric, meaning that you don't get the same shape
after rotations. When assembled together, they can form varied patterns depending on each tile's
orientation. Being the creative genius I am, I did look to the internet for help, but it was pretty amazing
to see intricate patterns emerge nothing from randomness. The one above uses tiles made out of
quarter circles and lines.



Ploygon SubDivision

During my fall quarter as a junior, I was browsing Pinterest for natural scenery when I eventually dived into a whole world
of farm landscapes. I was captivated by the recursive relationship of different plots of agriculture and the
repeating colors of each section. I wanted to reimagine this with code, so I found an algorithm that enabled me
to take a canvas and subdivide it several times along its width and height. With a dash of color here and some tweaks to
the striations, I ended up with this. Try it out with the link below. You'll notice that each iteration is unique and
and varies in their palette as well. I hope you find it fun and engaging to use!
